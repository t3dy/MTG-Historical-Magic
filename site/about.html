About the study

A digital-humanities word study of the vocabulary of magic — its origins, etymology, and history.

Magic: The Gathering has, over thirty years, become one of the largest published fantasy corpora in existence — tens of thousands of cards, each with a name, rules text, and a line or two of flavor. That makes it an unusually rich place to ask a linguistic question: what are all the words English uses for magic, and how does a modern fantasy world deploy them?

What we measure

Every term is counted morphologically — conjure, conjures, conjured, conjuring, conjurer, conjuration all collapse to one root, and the site shows you the spread of forms. Crucially, we split two very different surfaces:

  • Flavor text — the word in the story of a card: natural, literary language.
  • Rules text — the word as game mechanic: sometimes a formal keyword like Scry or Ward.

The distance between those two numbers is the whole point. Spell and transmute are almost purely mechanical; mage, curse, and necromancer are literary words the game tells stories with. The Insights chart plots every term along exactly this axis.

How it's built

FetchOne Scryfall bulk download of the whole corpus — never per-card scraping.
NormalizeFlatten to a tidy table: type line, oracle text, flavor text, images.
AnalyzeA curated regex lexicon of ~120 magical roots, matched morphologically.
IllustrateGallery images pulled from Scryfall for every card with the term in its title.

The whole pipeline is reproducible from a single command; the lexicon is hand-curated and human-reviewable, because deciding what counts as “a word for magic” is a scholarly judgement, not a statistical one.

The seven families

Practitioners (wizard, shaman, necromancer) · Acts (conjure, hex, enchant) · Divination (scry, augur, the whole -mancy clan) · Alchemy (transmute, elixir, azoth) · Qualities (arcane, occult, numinous) · Classical items — deliberately historical magic, not modern-fantasy coinage (talisman, sigil, phylactery, besom) · and History of the book (grimoire, codex, incunabulum) — because the book is itself a magical object.

Word Histories, grounded in the sources

The corpus tells us which words matter and gives us illustrations; to say where each word came from, the project draws on Ted's own databases and PDF library of medieval and renaissance magic and alchemy. A research tool (scripts/research.py) searches those databases (Renaissance definitions, MedievalMagicDB, the alchemy corpus) and the full-text scholarship — Klaassen, Fanger, Yates, the Picatrix, Lyndy Abraham's Dictionary of Alchemical Imagery — and returns a citable dossier for each term. Those become the Word Histories, and every term's Etymology tab auto-surfaces what the scholarship says.

Phylactery began as a Greek amulet and became a lich's soul-jar; glamour and grimoire are both mutations of the word “grammar”; the talisman of a mana-rock is the astral device of the Picatrix. Each is a page.

Deep time

All of it lands on one spine: the Timeline, a 300-entry history of the vocabulary, traditions, and practices of magic from Babylonian liver-divination to the day Scry became a keyword. Each entry is dated, sourced, tagged by kind — a word, a text, a figure, a tradition, a practice, or a game milestone — and, where the word still lives on a card, linked to its term page. It is the project's argument in one view: that the magic on a Magic card has a genealogy five thousand years deep.
